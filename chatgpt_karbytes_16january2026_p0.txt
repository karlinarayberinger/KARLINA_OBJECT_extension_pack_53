That plan is mostly solid, but one part is worth pushing back on a bit: aluminum foil isn’t a great long-term moisture barrier by itself, because it tears/pinholes easily and those tiny failures are enough for water vapor to get in over time. It’s fine as an extra layer, but don’t let it be the “primary defense.”

If you’re doing metal box + waterproof wrapping + spindle, the biggest upgrades (without changing your overall approach) are:

Spindle is fine, and it’s space-efficient. Just make sure the discs can’t rattle. Any movement + grit = scratches over years. Put something soft and inert in the voids (foam, cloth, bubble wrap).

Use plastic as the true barrier: 2–3 nested heavy-duty bags (like freezer-grade zip bags) or a real dry-bag / vacuum bag if you have one. Foil can go outside as abrasion/temperature shielding.

Desiccant matters more than foil: toss in several silica gel packs inside the innermost sealed layer.

Avoid “sealed off from air” assumptions: most consumer CD containers aren’t truly airtight. Treat them as physical protection, not an environmental seal.

Also, since you’re using a metal box, one caution: in wet soil, metal can eventually corrode. If the box is steel, consider adding an outer plastic layer around the whole box (even a thick contractor bag) so the soil isn’t directly contacting the metal.

Disc spindle → plastic bag + desiccant → second plastic bag → wrap/packing to prevent movement → metal box → outer plastic wrap/bag → buried
